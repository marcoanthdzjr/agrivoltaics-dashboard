1. Statistical Significance, Research Context & Weather Definitions

TMY, TDY, TGY & Historical Files

TMY (Typical Meteorological Year): Derived from 10-km by 10-km gridded NSRDB data. It is based on historical data but lacks real-time information and does not include extreme climatic events such as wildfires or volcanic eruptions.

TDY (Typical Direct Year): Developed using 100% weighting for direct normal irradiance (DNI).

TGY (Typical Global Year): Developed using 100% weighting for global horizontal irradiance (GHI).

Historical Actuals (CONUS vs. Aggregated): Real observed years that capture volatile extremes missed by the smoothed averages. CONUS files represent high-resolution, location-specific satellite data capturing extreme localized weather anomalies, whereas Aggregated files represent smoothed regional baselines.

Meteorological Variance in Agrivoltaic Design

The Mean (P50) represents expected revenue, but evaluating solely on the mean obfuscates the Variance. Variance exposes susceptibility to catastrophic tail-risks (P90). NREL research indicates that certain months, like May, demonstrate higher standard deviations for both DNI and GHI. For an agrivoltaic site to remain financially solvent, the physical layout must act as a reliable hedge during high-variance negative years by generating sufficient, stable cash flows.

2. Economic Structure: Single Owner PPA & TOD Parameters

To ensure strict parity with industry standards, the financial outcomes are structured around a Single Owner Power Purchase Agreement (PPA) model. This assumes the entity operates as an Independent Power Producer (IPP) selling 100% of generated power directly to the grid at wholesale/PPA rates, rather than a behind-the-meter commercial net-metering setup. Revenue is explicitly governed by the following Time-of-Delivery (TOD) multipliers against a standard base rate:

  • Base Rate: $0.12 / kWh
  • Summer Peak (June - September, 13:00 - 18:59 Weekdays): 3.5x Multiplier ($0.42 / kWh)
  • Winter Peak (December - February, 06:00 - 09:59 & 18:00 - 21:59 Weekdays): 2.0x Multiplier ($0.24 / kWh)
  • Off-Peak (All other hours & Weekends): 1.0x Multiplier ($0.12 / kWh)

Understanding the Marginal Variance

While the structural parameters have been strictly mirrored, a marginal mathematical variance remains between the two outputs. This is a deliberate and expected result of utilizing two distinct physics engines:

  • SAM Desktop (Detailed PV Model): Simulates at the microscopic level, calculating exact sub-hourly voltage and current limits across specific 550W module strings and discrete inverter threshold windows.
  • Python (PySAM PVWatts Engine): Simulates at the macroscopic level, utilizing a generalized systemic-efficiency envelope optimized for rapid, multi-year bulk weather processing.

Because of this fundamental difference in computational architecture, the Python outputs serve as a highly accurate relative sensitivity engine to map multi-year weather volatility, while the SAM Desktop targets serve as the absolute financial baseline.

8. Statistical Sensitivity Analysis: Drivers of Energy Yield

To mathematically prove exactly why these layouts react differently to atmospheric changes, a Pearson Correlation analysis was performed across the dataset. The chart below measures how strongly each meteorological variable influences the final energy yield, bounded between -1.0 (Strong Negative Impact) and +1.0 (Strong Positive Impact).

  • Irradiance (DNI vs. GHI): Dual Axis and Single Axis tracking systems demonstrate a near-perfect reliance (+1.0) on direct line-of-sight irradiance (DNI). Conversely, the Vertical Bifacial system relies much more heavily on Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) and ground albedo, buffering it against thick cloud cover that destroys DNI.
  • Thermal Degradation (Temperature): Notice the negative correlation across all layouts for Temperature. As ambient heat rises, the physical silicon cells experience a voltage drop, proving that extreme Texas heatwaves actively suppress solar generation despite clear skies.
  • Convective Cooling (Wind Speed): Higher wind speeds exhibit a slight positive correlation. As wind passes over the arrays, it strips away excess heat, naturally cooling the modules and mitigating the thermal degradation effect mentioned above.
